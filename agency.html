on agency

What agency means to me. The trait I optimize for the hardest.

Agency, to me, is just ownership. If you see something that needs doing, you do it. You don't wait, you don't check whose lane it's in, you don't ask for permission. You don't make yourself the victim of a situation you could change. Most people aren't lazy, they're just waiting -- for the right time, the green light, someone else to go first. Agentic people don't wait. They move, and then they figure it out.

If you can't take ownership wherever you are, that's not a job problem or a life problem. That's a you problem. Rethink why you're there.

at work

If a customer is stuck and they're technically not "mine", I'm still helping them. If something is broken and nobody owns it yet, I own it. If I think a thing should exist, I build the first ugly version of it instead of writing a doc proposing it. Trying and failing teaches you more in a week than asking for permission teaches you in a year.

I got my job at Gumloop this way. They weren't hiring. They weren't hiring remotely. They definitely weren't hiring someone from Pakistan. I cold DM'd the founder. Got ignored. DM'd again months later. Then again. Eventually he opened it. That's the job. There was no door, so I made one.

in personal life

Same rules. If I like someone, I say it. If a routine is killing me, I change it -- not next month, this week. If I want something, I go ask. The worst case is almost always a "no", and "no" is free.

Earlier this year I was working US overnight hours from Karachi and the days had collapsed into the same shape. Sleep, work, sleep, repeat. So I packed a bag and moved to Kuala Lumpur for a month. Then I did it again. Thailand and Indonesia are next. Nobody told me I could. Nobody was going to.

the shorthand

A few lines I actually live by:

  • A moving man meets his luck. Standing still is a decision, and it's usually the wrong one.
  • Shoot your shot. The ask you're scared to send is almost always the one worth sending.
  • Rules are mostly suggestions. Most people treat them like walls.
  • If you have to talk yourself into doing the thing, the thing is probably the move.
  • Action beats analysis. Try, fail, learn, repeat. That loop is the whole game.

This is the trait I trust most in myself and the one I look for hardest in other people. Everything else is downstream of it.
